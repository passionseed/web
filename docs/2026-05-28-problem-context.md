# Problem Context — PassionSeed

## Overview

This document captures the full problem context for PassionSeed, synthesized from market research, the workspace problem framework, pitch deck materials, B2B customer discovery, and an interview with a real student. The framing evolved from "one irreversible decision" to "a generation planning their future in the dark."

---

## 1. The Market

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| **TAM** | ~1.2 million Thai secondary students |
| **Lack guidance/testing** | 52% of Grade 9 students lack formal aptitude testing or structured career guidance |
| **Counselor ratio** | 400:1 student-to-counselor in Thai public schools |
| **Decision point** | Grade 9 (age 14–15) — track choice (Science / Arts / Vocational) |
| **Consequence** | Track choice locks university eligibility and career options for years |

---

## 2. The "One Decision" Trap (Original Framing)

At age 14–15, Thai students choose their high school track. This is **structural gatekeeping**, not a preference:

- **Science track** unlocks Medicine, Engineering, Architecture at top universities
- **Arts track** closes those doors entirely
- **Vocational** is often stigmatized

**The trap is de facto permanent.** Most students don't switch even when they know they chose wrong — switching means repeating a year, family shame, and financial cost. They white-knuckle through 3 years, arrive at university already defeated.

**Original problem statement:** Students are forced to choose a future before they've tested it.

---

## 3. The Deeper Problem (Evolved Framing)

The Grade 9 track choice is **not the root problem** — it's the **first visible symptom**. The deeper issue: **students don't know enough to answer basic questions about what they want.**

They cannot make informed decisions because they lack three things:

1. **No information** — No exposure to what careers actually involve day-to-day
2. **No self-awareness** — No framework to understand their own strengths, values, or working style
3. **No independent guidance** — Parents and counselors are biased, overwhelmed, or absent

---

## 4. The Interview Evidence

An interview with a real student (a friend) surfaced the exact mechanism:

| What she expected | What it actually is |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Creative thinking, campaigns, marketing | Media clipping, labor tasks at a PR group |
| Baking as a career = creative expression | Baking as a business = suppressing creativity |

**Key insight:** She chose based on the **label** ("creative" = Arts/Marketing) with zero understanding of the **labor**. The gap between the label and the labor is massive — and no one tells you until you're locked in.

---

## 5. The Card Game Insight

A diagnostic question reveals the depth of the problem:

> **"Suppose you have 4 Life Power Cards. Rank them by importance (1 = care most, 4 = don't care):"**
>
> 💰 **[Money Card]** = Must be rich, high salary, shopping, self-indulgence  
> 🛡️ **[Stability Card]** = Parents feel safe, no risk of unemployment, job until retirement  
> 🎨 **[Creativity Card]** = Use creative thinking, fun work, not bored, not drowning in documents/numbers  
> 🌿 **[Life Balance Card]** = Lots of free time, not overworked, time to travel, game, live your own life

**Students cannot answer this accurately.** Their rankings are distorted by three biases:

### Bias 1: Parent Bias
- Parents' career vocabulary: doctor, engineer, accountant, teacher
- They project a 1990s career landscape onto a 2030s future
- "Stable" means the same jobs their parents know
- Financial anxiety becomes career prescription — "stable" overrides "fits you"

### Bias 2: Zero Exposure
- Never spent a day with a professional in any field
- Career understanding = TV dramas + parents' job + TikTok
- No framework to connect aptitude tests to real paths
- Their understanding of "marketing" is TikTok creators, not media clipping

### Bias 3: No Self-Knowledge Framework
- Don't know their own working style
- Can't distinguish "I like this subject" from "I like the idea of this job"
- No language to explain their interests as legitimate paths
- Internalizing "it's not practical" as truth

---

## 6. Why This Matters

The problem is **not** that students make one bad choice. The problem is that they **cannot make any informed choice at all** because they lack the conceptual framework, the exposure, and the independent guidance to know what they want.

### The Consequences

| Consequence | Severity |
|-------------|----------|
| Stays in wrong track for 3 years | High |
| Locked out of right university | High |
| Unhappy, lost, disengaged | Medium |
| Wasted tuition and time | Medium |

---

## 7. The Personas Who Suffer

### Grade 9 — "The Anxious Achiever"
- Has good grades in everything, doesn't know what they're actually good at
- Everyone says Science, but they're not sure
- Seeks external validation (rankings, quizzes) but nothing feels certain

### Grade 9 — "The Pleaser"
- Quiet sense of what they want (art, writing, music) but suppresses it
- Family expects "stable" career
- Goes along to avoid conflict, disengages slowly

### Grade 9 — "The Drifter"
- No strong passion, no clear direction
- Chooses based on friends or gut feeling
- Never been asked what they actually care about

### Grade 10–11 — "The Regretful"
- Knew in month 2 that the track was wrong
- Stuck — switching means losing a year
- Parents won't hear it, disengages gradually

### Grade 10–11 — "The Anxious Achiever (Major)"
- Track is fine, but TCAS is coming and no idea which faculty
- Parents want Medicine, they're not sure
- Deep research spiral, paralyzed by comparison

### Grade 10–11 — "The Lost Creative"
- Passionate about game design, music, filmmaking — but no roadmap
- Told it's not a career, passion exists but has no visible path

### University — "The Mismatched Major"
- Enrolled in Medicine/Engineering based on past path
- Semester 1 confirmed the mismatch
- Too scared to drop out, too checked-out to perform

### University — "The Directionless Graduate"
- Degree done, no clarity on what job to take
- Applying broadly, hoping something sticks
- No one asks what kind of life they want — only what job

### University — "The Hidden Entrepreneur"
- Running side projects, freelancing, building quietly
- No validation, no network, no path forward
- Pressure to get a "real job" after graduation

## 10. Interview Validation Plan

| Priority | Who | Count | Goal |
|----------|-----|-------|------|
| 1 | Grade 9 student currently deciding | 5–8 | Validate decision experience in real time |
| 2 | Grade 10–11 student recently chose | 6–10 | Retrospective on regret, confidence, what was missing |
| 3 | Parent of Grade 9 student | 4–6 | Understand parental decision logic |
| 4 | University student year 1–2 | 4–6 | Validate long-term consequence |
| 5 | University student / young professional — wrong path, corrected | 3–5 | Understand cost of wrong choice |
| 6 | School counselor or homeroom teacher | 2–4 | Understand actual guidance capacity |

**Target:** 25–30 conversations total, 2–3 weeks for first round.

## 13. Key Design Principles

1. **The problem is not the decision — it's the lack of information to make any decision**
2. **The card game is not just a question — it's a diagnostic that reveals the depth of the problem**
3. **Students don't need more tests — they need exposure, frameworks, and independent voices**
4. **The "label vs. labor" gap is the specific mechanism that causes regret**
5. **The Grade 9 moment is the first visible symptom, not the root cause**
