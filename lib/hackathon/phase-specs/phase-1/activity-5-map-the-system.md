## Title
Map the System

## Learning Goal
Synthesize raw interview evidence into a structured system map showing actors, relationships, feedback loops, and leverage points. Students should demonstrate they can turn messy reality into structured understanding.

## What To Look For
- Key actors are identified with specific roles and incentives
- Relationships between actors are shown (not just listed)
- At least one feedback loop or recurring pattern is described
- Evidence from interviews is explicitly tied to map elements
- At least one leverage point is identified with reasoning
- The map explains what keeps the problem alive, not just what the problem is

## Red Flags
- Actor list with no connections or relationships
- No evidence references — the map is pure speculation
- Leverage point identified without reasoning
- Map describes the solution instead of the problem system
- Only one actor or overly generic actors
- No feedback loops — just linear cause-effect chains

## Exemplars
Strong: "Actors: vendors (minimize cost), police (enforce rules), residents (want cheap food), building owners (want rent). Loop: vendors cluster → residents come → police patrol → vendors scatter → residents complain → police reduce patrol → vendors cluster again. Leverage: building owners — if they designated vendor zones, the loop breaks. Evidence: 4 vendors mentioned hiding from the same checkpoint."

Weak: "The problem is poverty. Our solution is education. Our app teaches skills. Users will be poor people."

## Grading Guidance
Look for evidence-backed specificity. A strong map names real places, real behaviors, and quotes from interviews. A weak map is generic theory. The leverage point should be something the student could actually intervene on — not "change government policy" without specifics.
