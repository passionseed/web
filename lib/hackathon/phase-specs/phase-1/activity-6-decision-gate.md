## Title
Decision Gate: Proceed / Pivot / Kill

## Learning Goal
Make a reasoned decision under uncertainty using evidence. Students should demonstrate they can evaluate their own work honestly and decide whether to continue, change direction, or abandon based on evidence quality.

## What To Look For
- Clear decision: Proceed, Pivot, or Kill (not vague hedging)
- Decision is justified with specific evidence from prior activities
- Student identifies the strongest evidence supporting their decision
- Student acknowledges uncertainty or gaps honestly
- Student names what would change their mind
- Reasoning shows understanding of the problem system, not just the solution

## Red Flags
- Decision without evidence support ("we feel confident")
- Proceed decision with weak evidence
- Kill decision without having done real work
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty
- Cannot name what would change their mind
- Decision is purely about the solution's feasibility, not the problem's validity

## Exemplars
Strong: "We choose PROCEED. Strongest evidence: 6/7 riders described the same parking fine workaround, and 3 mentioned a specific soi they hide in. We understand the loop: riders park illegally → residents complain → police crack down → riders find new spots → cycle repeats. Leverage: designated parking zones could break the loop. What would change our mind: if interviews with residents show they actually prefer riders moving around (we only interviewed riders so far)."

Weak: "We choose proceed because we believe in our idea and we think it will help people. We are passionate about this problem."

## Grading Guidance
Reward intellectual honesty. A well-reasoned Kill decision with good evidence is stronger than a blind Proceed. Look for evidence references — the decision should read like a conclusion from the work they've done, not a hope. The "what would change our mind" test is critical: if they can't answer it, they haven't thought deeply enough.
