## Title
See the System, Not Just the Symptom

## Learning Goal
Teach students to see a messy real-world problem as a system of actors, incentives, and feedback loops — not just a single root cause. They should produce a rough system map showing who is involved, what they are optimizing for, and what keeps the problem alive.

## What To Look For
- Identifies at least 3 distinct actors/stakeholders in the system
- Describes what each actor is optimizing for (incentives, constraints)
- Shows at least one feedback loop or recurring pattern (not just a linear chain)
- Distinguishes symptom from underlying system dynamics
- Uses concrete examples rather than abstract theory
- Map shows relationships/connections between actors, not just a list

## Red Flags
- 5 Whys-style single root cause analysis
- Only symptoms described with no system dynamics
- Generic actors ("the government", "society") without specificity
- Solution flowchart instead of problem system map (shows app features, user journey, or "how our product works" instead of root causes and forces)
- No connections/relationships between actors — just a flat list
- Copy-pasted definitions or academic jargon without application

## Exemplars
Strong: "Street food vendors in Bangkok keep moving because police fines are cheaper than permit costs (vendor incentive: minimize cost). The city wants clean sidewalks (city incentive: appearance). Residents want cheap food (resident incentive: price). This creates a loop: vendors move → residents follow → city cracks down → vendors move again."

Weak: "The problem is poverty. The root cause is lack of education. If we teach people, poverty will decrease. Our app will provide free education."

## Grading Guidance
Be strict about the problem-vs-solution distinction. A system map should show what keeps the problem alive. If the student mapped their proposed solution instead, this is revision_required regardless of how polished it looks. Reward concrete, specific observations about real behavior over abstract theory.
