## 1. Executive Summary

Phase 1 should no longer teach a fake linear method for wicked problems.

This version uses **backward design**:
1. define what teams must be able to do by the end
2. define what evidence will prove they can do it
3. build only the activities required to produce that evidence

The phase is for **871 high school participants**. They do not need more busywork. They need a strong learning arc that helps them understand a real-world system, gather evidence from real people, identify leverage points, and decide.

Pedagogy starts from the end state: **Problem Proof Pack + Proceed / Pivot / Kill decision**. Everything in the phase must build toward that.

## 2. Why We Are Changing Direction

### What was wrong
- too linear
- too much task completion
- too much duplication
- too much "content about process" instead of real sensemaking
- 5 Whys is too narrow for wicked problems

### Core decision
We are explicitly dropping **5 Whys as a central method**.

For wicked problems, participants need to see: actors, incentives, bottlenecks, workarounds, feedback loops, unintended consequences, leverage points. That means **systems thinking**, not root-cause theater.

## 3. Backward Design Principle

1. **Desired results** — what should teams understand and be able to do?
2. **Acceptable evidence** — what artifacts or decisions will prove that understanding?
3. **Learning plan** — what activities are required to produce that evidence?

High school participants are especially vulnerable to confusing completion with understanding. The product should not reward them for "getting through the checklist." It should reward them for producing evidence that they understand a real problem inside a real system.

**Design rule:** If an activity does not produce evidence needed for the final decision, it should be cut, merged, or moved to optional support content.

## 4. Stage 1: Desired Results

### Transfer goal

By end of Phase 1, a team should be able to say:
> We understand the system around this problem, we have evidence from real people, we can explain what keeps the problem alive, and we know whether this is worth solving.

### End-of-phase capability

A successful team can:
- distinguish symptom from system
- explain how the problem currently works in the real world
- support claims with interview evidence instead of guesses
- identify at least one leverage point
- make a reasoned Proceed / Pivot / Kill decision

### Enduring understandings

- wicked problems are not solved by one clean root cause
- repeated behavior matters more than abstract opinions
- workarounds often reveal the real shape of the system
- a good problem statement is earned through evidence
- not every problem deserves a solution sprint

### Essential questions

1. What is happening in this system, not just at the surface?
2. Who is affected, and what are they optimizing for?
3. What patterns keep this problem alive?
4. What evidence proves this problem is real?
5. Is this a problem we should proceed with, pivot, or kill?

## 5. Stage 2: Acceptable Evidence

Phase 1 is successful only if teams leave with a **Problem Proof Pack** containing:
1. Target user
2. System map
3. Interview evidence
4. Problem statement
5. Proceed / Pivot / Kill decision

### Evidence standard

#### A. Systems understanding
- identify the relevant actors
- describe the sequence of events in the system
- identify incentives and constraints
- explain at least one loop or recurring pattern
- identify a plausible leverage point

#### B. Evidence quality
- gather 5-10 real interviews
- capture behavior, not just opinions
- surface recurring pain points and workarounds
- identify surprising or disconfirming findings

#### C. Problem clarity
- define a specific target user
- write a sharp, evidence-backed problem statement
- explain why this problem matters in context

#### D. Decision readiness
- decide Proceed / Pivot / Kill
- justify the decision with evidence and system understanding
- name what would change their mind

### Minimum rubric for mentors and reviewers

Judge on four dimensions:
1. **Reality** — grounded in real observed behavior?
2. **Systems understanding** — does the team understand what keeps the problem alive?
3. **Specificity** — is user, context, and problem statement sharp enough?
4. **Decision quality** — is Proceed / Pivot / Kill justified by evidence?

### Assessment rule
Every core activity should produce one of: orientation toward end goal, raw evidence, synthesized evidence, decision-ready reflection, or a required final artifact. Otherwise it is not core Phase 1 pedagogy.

## 6. Stage 3: Learning Plan (7 core activities max)

### Activity 1 — What You'll Walk Away With
- **Purpose:** Set the promise and frame the phase correctly.
- **Key message:** You are not here to find an idea quickly. You are here to understand a system well enough to choose a meaningful problem.
- **Format:** Webtoon / comic / short intro visual.
- **Output:** Orientation only. No graded artifact.

### Activity 2 — See the System, Not Just the Symptom
- **Purpose:** Teach a simple systems-thinking lens before interviews begin.
- **Look for:** who is involved, sequence of events, friction, workarounds, incentives, what happens when someone tries to fix it, loops/recurring patterns.
- **Format:** Interactive lesson or guided tool walkthrough.
- **Preferred tool:** Loopy or similar causal loop mapping.
- **Output:** Rough System Map v1.

### Activity 3 — Interview Real Humans
- **Purpose:** Gather evidence from reality, not opinions.
- **Principle:** Do not teach 5 Whys. Teach students to trace the system.
- **Question patterns focus on:** what happened last time, step by step, who else was involved, difficulties, workaround, tradeoff, what happened after, what made the problem continue.
- **Avoid:** leading questions, pitching solutions, abstract opinions without behavior.
- **Output:** 5-10 real interviews. Core raw dataset.

### Activity 4 — Upload Evidence
- **Purpose:** Force teams to ground claims in evidence that can be reviewed.
- **Accepted inputs:** interview notes, clips, audio, observations, field-note screenshots.
- **Output:** Evidence bundle.

### Activity 5 — Map the System
- **Purpose:** Turn raw evidence into structure.
- **Team produces:** key actors, recurring pain points, existing workarounds, incentives/constraints, reinforcing/balancing loops, possible leverage points.
- **Output:** System Map v2.

### Activity 6 — Decision Gate: Proceed / Pivot / Kill
- **Purpose:** Make the team decide what the evidence means.
- **Options:** Proceed (strong evidence, clear leverage) · Pivot (real problem, wrong framing/user) · Kill (weak evidence or low-value).
- **Required reflection:**
  1. What is the strongest evidence this problem is real?
  2. What keeps this problem alive in the system?
  3. Where is the leverage point?
  4. Why are we proceeding, pivoting, or killing?
  5. What evidence would make us change our mind?

### Activity 7 — Submit Problem Proof Pack
- **Required contents:**
  1. **Target User** — who specifically, context, why this group matters
  2. **System Map** — actors, loops, bottlenecks, leverage point
  3. **Evidence Summary** — 5-10 interviews, strongest patterns, surprising findings, workarounds observed
  4. **Problem Statement** — specific, evidence-backed, grounded in system behavior
  5. **Decision** — Proceed / Pivot / Kill with reasoning

## 7. Pedagogical Guardrails

### What this phase should teach
- how to see a messy real-world problem as a system
- how to gather behavioral evidence
- how to synthesize evidence into structure
- how to make a reasoned decision under uncertainty

### What this phase should not teach
- that discovery is a neat checklist
- that one "root cause" explains a wicked problem
- that confidence matters more than evidence
- that a team must proceed just because they started

### Pedagogical stance

This phase should feel like: concrete · evidence-first · slightly demanding · grounded in reality · oriented toward judgment.

It should not feel like: founder theater · abstract innovation language · fake rigor · academic systems theory.

## 8. Optional Support Workflow

### Mentor Review
Mentor review is optional as a learning activity and should be treated as support and evaluation workflow, not core pedagogy. If included, it should come **after** the Problem Proof Pack so mentors are reacting to evidence and decision quality, not replacing the team's own judgment.

## 9. What We Are Cutting

Remove from core Phase 1:
- Mascot 5-Whys
- 5 Whys as a named methodology
- separate persona-first exercise before evidence exists
- duplicate AI feedback steps
- NotebookLM guideline as a required activity
- trustful sources submission as a separate learning milestone
- mentor booking as a core learning activity

These may be: deleted · merged · moved to optional support · moved to later phases.

## 10. New Learning Model

- **Old model:** Problem → ask why → find root cause → define problem
- **New model:** Messy situation → understand the system → gather evidence → find loops and leverage points → define the right problem → decide whether to continue
- **Backward-designed model:** Desired outcome → required evidence → learning experiences → final decision

This is the core pedagogical shift.

## 11. Loopy / Systems Mapping Tool Requirements

Participants should be able to: name actors and elements, connect cause/effect relationships, show reinforcing loops, show balancing loops, mark bottlenecks, identify possible intervention points.

**Important constraint:** Do **not** turn this into formal systems theory. Students do not need academic vocabulary. They need practical prompts:
- What keeps this problem going?
- Who is stuck in it?
- What are they optimizing for?
- What workaround makes it worse?
- Where could a small intervention change a lot?

## Grading guidance (for AI grader)

**Pass** requires:
- claims backed by interview evidence (not assumption)
- distinguishes symptom from underlying system
- identifies at least one actor / incentive / feedback loop
- clear Proceed / Pivot / Kill decision with reasoning
- target user is specific, not a demographic generalization
- system map shows actors + at least one loop

**Needs revision** signals:
- checklist-style "completion" without sensemaking
- 5 Whys-style root cause theater
- no real-world evidence, or fewer than 5 interviews with nothing surprising
- conflates opinion with observation
- generic target user ("students", "everyone")
- decision without evidence support
- founder theater / abstract innovation language
- fake rigor or academic systems theory vocabulary without substance

**Pending** when submission is incomplete or unclear.

Reward evidence-grounded sensemaking. Be kind but rigorous. These are high schoolers learning the difference between checklist completion and real understanding. Do not reward fast completion.
