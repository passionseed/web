## 1. Executive Summary

Phase 2 teaches high school teams how to move from a validated problem to a tested solution — without building the real thing first.

This version uses **backward design**:
1. define what teams must be able to do by the end
2. define what evidence will prove they can do it
3. build only the activities required to produce that evidence

The phase is for **871 high school participants** who have completed Phase 1 with a "Proceed" decision. They do not need more theory. They need to learn that the fastest way to learn is to test without building.

The core loop:

**hypothesis → pretotype → test → decide → build (only if validated)**

Everything in the phase must build toward the end state:

**Validated Learning Pack + Persevere/Pivot/Kill decision**

---

## 2. Why This Approach

### What teams struggle with after Phase 1
- They jump to the first obvious solution
- They start building before validating demand
- They confuse "I have an idea" with "people want this"
- They don't know how to test without coding

### Core decision
We are explicitly teaching **pretotyping before prototyping**.

For high schoolers with no coding experience, the "build" phase should start with the smallest possible test: a Google Form, a manual chatbot, a fake landing page. Only after validation do they touch AI Studio.

---

## 3. Backward Design Principle

1. **Desired results** — what should teams understand and be able to do?
2. **Acceptable evidence** — what artifacts or decisions will prove that understanding?
3. **Learning plan** — what activities are required to produce that evidence?

High school participants are especially vulnerable to confusing building with progress. The product should not reward them for "making something." It should reward them for "learning something."

**Design rule:** If an activity does not produce evidence needed for the final decision, it should be cut, merged, or moved to optional support content.

---

## 4. Stage 1: Desired Results

### Transfer goal

By end of Phase 2, a team should be able to say:
> We tested our solution idea without building it, we have evidence that people want it (or don't), and we either have a validated prototype or we know why to pivot.

### End-of-phase capability

A successful team can:
- write a testable hypothesis from a problem statement
- choose and build a pretotype appropriate to their problem
- run a real test with users and collect data
- make a Persevere/Pivot/Kill decision based on evidence
- build a high-fidelity prototype (if Persevere)

### Enduring understandings

- The fastest way to learn is to test without building
- A "no" from a pretotype test is as valuable as a "yes"
- Building before validating is expensive waste
- Data beats gut feeling for early decisions
- Not every good idea deserves to be built

### Essential questions

1. What do we believe about our solution, and how would we know if we're wrong?
2. What's the smallest thing we could build to test this belief?
3. Who did we test with, and what did they actually do?
4. What does the data tell us — Persevere, Pivot, or Kill?
5. Did we earn the right to build a real prototype?

---

## 5. Stage 2: Acceptable Evidence

Phase 2 is successful only if teams leave with a **Validated Learning Pack** containing:

1. **Testable hypothesis** — "We believe [user] needs [solution] because [evidence]"
2. **Pretotype description** — what they built, why this method, how it tests the hypothesis
3. **Test results** — who they tested with, what happened, data collected
4. **Decision** — Persevere/Pivot/Kill with evidence-based reasoning
5. **High-fidelity prototype** (if Persevere) — working AI-powered solution

### Evidence standard

#### A. Hypothesis clarity
- Hypothesis is specific and testable
- Tied to Phase 1 Problem Proof Pack evidence
- Includes who, what, and why

#### B. Pretotype fit
- Method matches the hypothesis
- Actually tests the core belief
- Can be built in under 2 hours

#### C. Evidence quality
- Tested with at least 5 real users (not teammates)
- Data is specific, not vague ("3 out of 5 signed up" not "people liked it")
- Captures behavior, not just opinions

#### D. Decision quality
- Persevere/Pivot/Kill is justified by data
- Team can name what would change their mind
- Not driven by attachment to the idea

#### E. Prototype quality (if Persevere)
- Works end-to-end
- Built with Google AI Studio
- Demonstrates the core value proposition

### Minimum rubric for mentors and reviewers

Judge on four dimensions:
1. **Hypothesis clarity** — Is it specific, testable, and tied to Phase 1 evidence?
2. **Pretotype fit** — Does the method actually test the hypothesis?
3. **Evidence quality** — Did they test with real users? Is the data specific?
4. **Decision quality** — Is Persevere/Pivot/Kill justified by data?

### Assessment rule

Every core activity should produce one of: orientation toward the end goal, raw evidence, synthesized evidence, decision-ready reflection, or a required final artifact.

## 6. Stage 3: Learning Plan (5 core activities)

### Activity 1 — From Problem to Hypothesis

**Backward-design role:** Produce the first required evidence — a testable hypothesis.

**Purpose:** Convert Phase 1 Problem Proof Pack into a testable hypothesis.

**Key message:** Before you build anything, write down what you believe and how you'd know if you're wrong.

**Format:** Guided worksheet with team discussion prompts.

**Prompts:**
- We believe [type of user] needs [solution] because [evidence from Phase 1]
- We will know we are right if [specific observable outcome]
- We will know we are wrong if [specific observable outcome]

**Output:** Testable hypothesis artifact.

**Evidence value:** Orientation + required evidence #1.

---

### Activity 2 — Choose Your Pretotype

**Backward-design role:** Produce the second required evidence — a selected test method with justification.

**Purpose:** Learn pretotyping methods, pick one that fits the problem.

**Key message:** You don't need to build the real thing to learn. You need to build the smallest thing that tests your belief.

**Methods taught:**

| Method | When to use | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Wizard of Oz** | Test service/experience | Human pretends to be AI chatbot behind a simple interface |
| **Fake Door** | Test demand/interest | Landing page with "sign up" button that goes to a waitlist |
| **Concierge** | Test full workflow | Manually deliver the service to a few users |
| **Pinocchio** | Test physical/product | Non-functional prototype that looks real |

**Format:** Interactive explainer with examples per method.

**Team produces:** Selected method + one-sentence justification ("We chose Fake Door because we need to test if people will sign up before we build anything").

**Output:** Method selection artifact.

**Evidence value:** Required evidence #2.

---

### Activity 3 — Build the Pretotype

**Backward-design role:** Produce the third required evidence — a working test artifact.

**Purpose:** Create the minimum viable test.

**Key message:** This should take hours, not days. If it's taking too long, you're building too much.

**Tools by method:**
- **Wizard of Oz:** Google Forms, simple chat UI, manual responses
- **Fake Door:** Canva landing page, Google Form waitlist, QR code
- **Concierge:** Google Sheets, phone/WhatsApp, manual delivery
- **Pinocchio:** Canva mockups, printed materials, roleplay script

**Format:** Step-by-step build guide per method. Include time limits (2 hours max).

**Anti-build guardrails:**
- No coding required
- No AI Studio yet
- Must be testable today

**Output:** Working pretotype ready to test.

**Evidence value:** Required evidence #3.

---

### Activity 4 — Run the Test + Decide

**Backward-design role:** Produce the fourth required evidence — test results + decision with reasoning.

**Purpose:** Test with real users, collect data, make decision.

**Key message:** The goal is not to prove you're right. The goal is to learn.

**Test requirements:**
- At least 5 real users (not teammates, not family)
- Record what they did, not what they said
- Capture specific numbers: sign-ups, clicks, time spent, completion rate

**Decision framework:**

| Decision | Meaning | Next step |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Persevere** | Strong evidence, clear demand | Proceed to Activity 5 (build high-fi prototype) |
| **Pivot** | Problem is real, solution is wrong | Book mentor session to refine approach |
| **Kill** | Weak evidence or low demand | Book mentor session to explore new problem |

**Required reflection:**
1. What is the strongest evidence from our test?
2. What surprised us?
3. What would make us change our decision?
4. Why are we choosing Persevere/Pivot/Kill?

**Branching:**
- **Persevere → Activity 5**
- **Pivot or Kill → Book mentor session** (optional support to restart or refine)

**Output:** Test results + Persevere/Pivot/Kill decision with evidence.

**Evidence value:** Required evidence #4.

---

### Activity 5 — Build High-Fidelity Prototype

**Backward-design role:** Produce the fifth required evidence — but ONLY for teams that earned it through validation.

**Purpose:** Create a working prototype using Google AI Studio.

**Key message:** You earned the right to build. Now make something that works.

**When:** ONLY for teams that chose "Persevere" in Activity 4.

**Anti-skip guard:** Teams cannot access Activity 5 without a "Persevere" decision in Activity 4.

**Format:** Guided tutorial with templates.

**Templates:**
- Health chatbot (for mental health / wellness solutions)
- Info assistant (for education / awareness solutions)
- Screening tool (for diagnostic / preventive solutions)
- Tracker (for monitoring / behavior change solutions)

**Output:** Working AI-powered prototype.

**Evidence value:** Required evidence #5 (conditional).

## 7. Pedagogical Guardrails

### What this phase should teach
- How to test a belief without building the full solution
- How to choose the right test for the right question
- How to collect data that actually answers your question
- How to kill an idea gracefully when the data says no
- How to build a prototype only after validation

### What this phase should not teach
- That building is progress
- That every idea deserves a prototype
- That gut feeling beats data
- That coding is required to test
- That pivoting is failure

### Pedagogical stance

This phase should feel like:
- concrete
- fast
- slightly uncomfortable (killing ideas is hard)
- evidence-first
- oriented toward learning, not building

It should not feel like:
- founder theater
- "build it and they will come"
- abstract startup theory
- coding bootcamp

---

## 8. Optional Support Workflow

### Mentor Review

Mentor review is optional as a learning activity and should be treated as support and evaluation workflow, not core pedagogy.

If included, it should come **after** the Validated Learning Pack so mentors are reacting to evidence and decision quality, not replacing the team's own judgment.

### Mentor Booking for Pivot/Kill Teams

Teams that choose Pivot or Kill in Activity 4 can book a mentor session to:
- Refine their hypothesis and try a new pretotype
- Explore whether their Phase 1 problem needs re-examination
- Get guidance on selecting a new problem from the challenge set

This is **support**, not a gate. Teams can also choose to proceed on their own.

## 10. New Learning Model

### Old model
Problem → come up with idea → build prototype → hope it works

### New model
Problem → write hypothesis → build smallest test → run test → decide with data → build only if validated

### Backward-designed model
Desired outcome (validated learning) → required evidence (hypothesis + pretotype + test results + decision) → learning experiences → final decision

---

## 11. Pretotyping Tool Requirements

Participants should be able to:
- create a simple landing page (Canva or Google Sites)
- set up a Google Form with conditional logic
- create a QR code linking to their test
- manually respond to messages (Wizard of Oz)
- track responses in a simple spreadsheet

**Important constraint:** Do not require coding. Do not require design skills. The tools should be things they already use or can learn in 10 minutes.

## 15. Success Criteria

Phase 2 succeeds if:
- teams can write a testable hypothesis from their Problem Proof Pack
- teams can explain what a pretotype is and why it matters
- teams build and run at least one pretotype test with 5+ real users
- teams make a Persevere/Pivot/Kill decision based on data, not gut feeling
- teams that Persevere have a working high-fidelity prototype
- teams that Pivot or Kill can book a mentor session for support
- mentors can assess whether a team validated before building
- teams cannot skip pretotyping to jump to high-fi prototype

## Grading guidance (for AI grader)

**Pass** requires:
- hypothesis is specific and testable
- pretotype method matches the hypothesis
- test includes at least 5 real users
- decision references specific data points
- high-fi prototype works end-to-end (if Persevere)

**Needs revision** signals:
- hypothesis is vague or untestable
- pretotype doesn't actually test the hypothesis
- no real users tested
- decision is gut-based, not data-based
- skipped pretotyping and jumped to building

**Auto-fail:**
- no pretotype built
- no test run
- decision made without any data

Reward evidence-grounded learning. Be kind but rigorous. These are high schoolers learning that killing an idea quickly is better than building the wrong thing slowly.
